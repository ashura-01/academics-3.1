
# INTRODUCTION TO ECONOMICS

## 1. Origin and Definition of Economics

- **Origin of the word:** The term "`Economics`" comes from the Greek word "`Oikonomos`" which means *"one who manages a household."*
- **Standard Definition:** Economics is the **social science** that studies how societies use **scarce resources** to produce valuable goods and services and distribute them among different people.

---

## 2. Core Concepts (The Building Blocks)

### A. Scarcity (The Fundamental Problem)

- **Definition:** Scarcity is the condition where **unlimited human wants** exceed the **limited resources** available to satisfy those wants.

- **Key Point:** If scarcity did not exist, we wouldn't need economics. Economics is essentially the study of how we deal with scarcity.

### B. Goods vs. Bads

- **Goods:** Anything that provides **utility** (satisfaction or happiness). 
  - *Tangible (physical):* Car, computer, food.
  - *Intangible (non-physical):* Friendship, love, health.
  
- **Bads:** Anything that provides **disutility** (dissatisfaction or unhappiness). 
  - *Examples:* The flu, pollution, constant nagging.
  
- **Key Point:** People will pay money to get *goods* (e.g., buy medicine) and will also pay money to get rid of *bads* (e.g., pay a doctor to cure the flu).

### C. Choice (The Direct Result of Scarcity)
- Because resources are limited (scarcity exists), people **must make choices**. We have to decide which wants to satisfy and which to ignore.

### D. Opportunity Cost (The True Cost of a Choice)
- **Definition:** The **opportunity cost** of an item is the **value of the next best alternative** that you give up when making a choice.
- **Crucial Rule:** Every single choice has an opportunity cost.
- **Example:** If you choose to attend this economics lecture, your opportunity cost is *not* the tuition fee. It is the value of the next best thing you gave up (e.g., watching TV, taking a nap, or working a part-time job).

### E. Efficiency (The Ultimate Goal)
- **Definition:** Efficiency means making the **most effective use** of a society's resources to satisfy people's wants.
- **Economic Efficiency:** An economy achieves this when it produces the highest possible combination of quality and quantity of goods using its current technology and resources.
- **The Test for Efficiency:** An economy is perfectly efficient when **you cannot make one person better off without making another person worse off.**

## 3. The Two Main Divisions of Economics

| **Microeconomics (The "Zoomed In" View)** | **Macroeconomics (The "Big Picture" View)** |
| :--- | :--- |
| Studies **individual** economic units. | Studies the **aggregate** (total) economy. |
| Focuses on individuals, single markets, households, and firms. | Focuses on national income, total employment, and overall price levels. |
| **Key Questions:** <br> - How is the price of a single good determined? <br> - What output level should a firm produce? <br> - How does a consumer decide what to buy? <br> - How do government policies affect a specific industry (e.g., tax breaks for farmers)? | **Key Questions:** <br> - What causes inflation (rising prices)? <br> - Why does the unemployment rate go up and down? <br> - Why do some countries grow faster than others? <br> - How do changes in government spending/taxes affect the whole nation? |
| **Real-World Policy Example (Bangladesh):** <br> Giving tax exemptions (TIN/BIN) to individual digital creators on YouTube/TikTok. | **Real-World Policy Example (Bangladesh):** <br> Increasing national development expenditure by 47% for infrastructure, or adopting flexible exchange rates to strengthen foreign reserves. |

## 4. The Three Fundamental Economic Questions

**Every society, regardless of its political system, must answer these three questions:**

### Question 1: WHAT to produce?
- **Meaning:** Deciding which goods/services to make and in what quantities.
- **Why it matters:** Producing more of one thing means producing less of another (due to scarcity).
- **Illustrative Example:** A software company has limited developers. It must choose whether to build a **mobile banking app** OR a **hospital management system**. It cannot do both, so it chooses the one with the highest demand.

### Question 2: HOW to produce?
- **Meaning:** Deciding the most efficient method of production. 
- **Why it matters:** Firms want to minimize costs while maintaining quality.
- **Illustrative Example:** After deciding to build the banking app, the company must choose between using **traditional human coding** OR using **AI-assisted automation tools** to get the job done faster and cheaper.

### Question 3: FOR WHOM to produce?
- **Meaning:** Deciding who will receive the finished goods or services. (i.e., who is the target customer?).
- **Why it matters:** Resources are limited, so you must target specific segments of society.
- **Illustrative Example:** The company releases its project management software. Will it offer a **free, basic version for students** OR a **premium, expensive version for large corporations**?
